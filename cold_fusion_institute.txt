Introduction
On March 23, 1989, Martin Fleischmann and Stanley Pons, two chemists who were working at the University of Utah in Salt Lake City, Utah, announced that they had produced a nuclear fusion reaction in a process that became known as cold fusion.[1] The two said that they had conducted an experiment where an electric current was passed between two electrodes made of palladium submerged in heavy water which had produced a large amount of heat that they attributed to fusion of the atomic nuclei of the deuterium present in the water.[1]

By early April, researchers at several American universities, including the Georgia Institute of Technology, Texas A&M University, and the University of Washington, reported that similar experiments had resulted in some evidence of nuclear fusion having occurred.[1] However, these reports were all subsequently withdrawn after various errors in the experiments were discovered.[1] Further research at numerous institutes and universities was unable to demonstrate that fusion was occurring.[1] On May 1, the American Physical Society held a meeting where cold fusion was largely dismissed, and on November 12, a committee of the United States Department of Energy concluded that cold fusion was unproven and did not merit further research.[2]

Additionally, Fleischmann's and Pon's results were criticized by other researchers,[1][3] with scientists working for General Electric publishing a paper in 1992 that said that the two had misinterpreted the data they had collected from their experiments.[4] In November 1989, researchers from the Harwell Laboratory published a report in the scientific journal Nature where they said they were unable to replicate Fleischmann's and Pon's results and speculated that the two's claims may have been the result of errors in their experimentation.[3] Furthermore, in January 1991, scientist Frank Close wrote in New Scientist that, "my own researches during the past 18 months show that some of the so-called evidence for fusion was not obtained and presented to the world in the accepted scientific way".[1]

Establishment
On March 24, 1989—the day after Fleischmann and Pons made their initial announcements concerning cold fusion—Utah Governor Norman H. Bangerter announced a special session of the Utah State Legislature in order to discuss state funding for cold fusion research.[2] On April 7, the legislature passed legislation to allocate $5 million in funding towards cold fusion.[5][4] Of this amount, $4.5 million (equivalent to $11.7 million in 2025) would go towards the establishment and operation of a research organization,[6] while $500,000 ($1.3 million in 2025) would go towards paying patent attorneys.[7] Ultimately, the government of Utah established the National Cold Fusion Institute (NCFI), a nonprofit research institute,[8] which officially opened on August 7, 1989.[2][9]

The organization would be affiliated with the university,[10] which was also operated by the state government,[6] and based in the University of Utah Research Park,[7] at 390 Wakara Way.[11][12] For this location, the university signed a five-year lease for $1.1 million ($2.9 million in 2025).[5][4] In addition to the state funding, the institute was hopeful that additional private funding could be secured in the future,[6] and the institute also lobbied the United States Congress for $25 million ($65 million in 2025) in federal funding.[1] According to the Deseret News, at the time, the institute was only one of two instances of funding being allocated for cold fusion research, alongside funding allocated to several universities by the Electric Power Research Institute (EPRI).[7] Prior to the institute's establishment, the university acquired the rights to use the term "cold fusion" from Jim Bradley, a Democratic Party politician who had previously served as the head of the state's Department of Energy.[13] Bradley had registered the term with the state's Department of Commerce on April 5 for $20 ($52 in 2025) and sold the rights to use the term to the university for $2,000 ($5,200 in 2025).[13]

The institute's funding appropriations would be overseen by the Fusion/Energy Advisory Council, which would consist of members appointed by the state government.[14] Raymond L. Hixson served as the council's chairman,[15] being appointed to that position by Governor Bangerter roughly two weeks after Fleischmann's and Pons's initial announcements.[16] Hugo Rossi, the dean of the University of Utah College of Science, served as the institute's first director.[6] According to Rossi, officials associated with the institute initially considered other people for the role of director, including chemist John Bockris of Texas A&M, before deciding on him.[7] Concerning the composition of the institute's board of trustees, university officials stated that they hoped to appoint several businessman alongside academics, with Ian Cumming, James C. Fletcher, and Jon Huntsman Sr. mentioned by the Deseret News as possible candidates.[17] Initially, Pons served only as an advisor for the institute and continued his own research into cold fusion independently.[7] However, in October, both Fleischmann and Pons joined the NCFI with the condition that their work would be peer reviewed by other members of the institute.[18]

Early activities
In late September 1989, Rossi stated that none of the experiments conducted by the institute up to that time had yielded results that would indicate a nuclear fusion reaction was taking place,[19][5] such as the production of excess heat, neutrons, or tritium.[20] Rossi initially stated that the institute may close if no results were found by February 1990, though he later stated that the institute had no deadline for finding results.[5] On October 1, the institute announced that its facilities would be off-limits to reporters and that university officials James Brophy and Pam Fogle would serve as the official spokespeople for the institute and issue occasional press releases.[20] Around the same time, the Westinghouse Electric Corporation publicly announced that they would not be moving forward with requests from the institute to help fund cold fusion research.[21] However, later that month, Brophy stated that he was confident that the institute would attract between 10 and 20 private investors by the end of the fiscal year.[22]

In November, a panel of 22 scientists from the United States Department of Energy's Energy Research Advisory Board presented a report advising against the allocation of the $25 million in federal funding that the institute had requested.[23] The panel, headed by John R. Huizenga, had been studying cold fusion since May and reported that it had found no concrete evidence of fusion reactions in any of the dozens of experiments that had been conducted throughout the United States.[23] Also in November, Rossi resigned as director after four months in the position.[6] Speaking to New Scientist in June 1990 regarding his resignation, he said, "Part of the reason for quitting was the inability to get the personnel there to collaborate", further stating that Pons refused to share data with other scientists on several occasions.[6] The following month, Fritz Will, a research scientist from General Electric,[24] was appointed the new director of the institute.[25] In the interim, Brophy had served as the institute's acting director.[24]

In late March 1990,[4][5] around the anniversary of Fleischmann's and Pon's initial announcement, the institute held its first annual conference in Salt Lake City to discuss the state of cold fusion research, which was attended by about 200 people.[26][27] Forty people presented papers at the conference.[26] Around the same time, a team of researchers led by Michael Salaman[note 1] of the University of Utah published a paper in Nature that included the results of studies they had conducted on the apparatus used by Pons where they claimed that they had found no evidence of cold fusion.[26] Pons responded that one of the cells used in his experiments had produced excess heat for two hours, but that it had occurred during a power failure when the team's detectors were not working.[26] At the time, Will said that researchers at the institute were attempting to optimize an apparatus that he said was producing excess heat.[26]

Legal and funding controversies
On April 3, 1990, C. Gary Triggs,[29] an attorney representing Fleischmann and Pons, sent a letter to Salaman demanding that he retract the paper that had recently been published by Nature.[6] In the letter, Triggs wrote, "Please be advised that any damages suffered by my clients proximately caused by any act or omission on the part of yourself or any other coauthor of the subject paper will not be tolerated. I have been instructed by my clients to take such legal action as is deemed appropriate to protect their interests in this matter."[6] Salaman also stated that Chase N. Peterson, the president of the University of Utah, had instructed him to "lie low" and that the university had initially told him that it would refuse to indemnify him in the case of a lawsuit.[6] However, Joseph L. Taylor, the university's vice president of academic affairs, later told Salaman that the state government would defend him for lawsuits filed against him for his work.[6] In a later article by New Scientist, Salaman noted that, at the time, Triggs had been paid $68,000 ($168,000 in 2025) by the university as a consultant for patents on cold fusion.[6] In response to the letter by Triggs, the American Physical Society stated that they would discuss the matter at their next executive meeting, while society member Robert L. Park stated that some members were offering to contribute to a legal defense fund for Salaman.[6] Triggs later apologized for the letter.[28]

In early 1990, Peterson announced that an anonymous donor had made a donation of $500,000 ($1.2 million in 2025) to the institute.[6] However, on May 31,[2] Peterson stated that the donor had actually been the university itself.[6] The revelation was a surprise to the Fusion/Energy Advisory Council, whose members had believed that the donation had been evidence that the institute was succeeding in generating outside interest from investors.[14] Following the disclosure, Will declined the donation.[6] Per Rossi, who at the time was the dean of the College of Science, the donation was "an attempt" by the president "to bring less controversy to the institute", though that had backfired.[6] On June 1, 22 members of the College of Science held a meeting to discuss the incident, with the members informing Peterson that they would be forming a panel to investigate the research that the institute had conducted to date.[6] By that time the institute had spent roughly $2.5 million ($6.2 million in 2025) of its funding.[6] On June 1, the Deseret News reported that Peterson would retire at the end of the 1990–1991 academic year.[28] Several days later, on June 4, the university's faculty senate voted to ask the Utah Board of Higher Education to investigate Peterson over his competency for the position of president.[6] Also in early June, the EPRI, which had announced interest in signing a contract that would have bestowed the NCFI with a grant of $160,000 ($394,000 in 2025), opted against entering into a relationship with the NCFI.[6]

Financial audit and scientific review
In June 1990, the Fusion/Energy Advisory Council announced that, at the behest of faculty members from the College of Science, they would be initiating both a financial audit and scientific review of the institute.[30] In late October, the Utah State Auditor's office completed their financial audit of the NCFI.[31] Tom Allen, the state auditor, said that they had made several recommendations to the institute in a letter sent to Will on October 24.[31] He also stated that the university would have about two weeks to respond to the recommendations before the audit would be made public.[31] Ultimately, the audit found several violations of the university's standard procedures,[32] but no "material weaknesses" in the institute's financial handlings.[33] By the end of October, The Salt Lake Tribune reported that the institute had received $40,000 ($99,000 in 2025) in private funding.[34]

The state's Fusion/Energy Advisory Council stated in October that they would hold their scientific review of the institute on November 7.[35][4] By this time, only $1.3 million ($3.2 million in 2025) remained from the initial $5 million ($13 million in 2025) that had been allocated for the institute and patent attorneys.[35] Over the previous fiscal year, $2.1 million ($5.2 million in 2025) in state funding had been allocated to cold fusion research, with Fleischmann and Pons having spent $1 million ($2.5 million in 2025) of those funds.[35] (Pons later stated that he and Fleischmann had only spent $325,000 ($801,000 in 2025) of NCFI funds on research, though Will refuted this figure.)[36] However, neither man had published a scientific paper on their research or shared their research results with other institute members.[35] Both Will and council members expressed disappointment over the two's lack of cooperation, with Karen Morse—provost at Utah State University and one of the two scientists on the council—saying, "In essence, we've coddled them, quite frankly".[35] By late October, it was uncertain whether the two men would cooperate with the review.[35]

On October 25, Will and several researchers, excluding Fleischmann and Pons, attended a meeting with the council where they presented summaries of their results.[37] The researchers noted that in several experiments they had observed tritium levels that were slightly higher than background levels, though Douglas Morrison, a physicist from CERN who was present at the review, noted that the amounts were too small to indicate that fusion had occurred.[37]

On November 7, a panel of four scientists—Robert Adair, a Sterling Professor of physics at Yale University; Stanley Bruckenstein, a chemistry professor at the State University of New York at Buffalo; Loren G. Helper, a chemistry and chemical engineering professor at the University of Alberta; and Dale F. Stein, a materials scientist and president of Michigan Technological University—met with NCFI researchers, including Pons, to begin their review,[38] which they were planning to publicly release in early January 1991.[39] Despite the fact that the panel members had signed confidentiality agreements, Fleischmann and Pons, citing advice from a patent attorney, did not fully disclose their research to the panel.[5] The panel ultimately concluded that, while the research seemed solid, they had been unable to find evidence that nuclear fusion had occurred in the researchers' experiments.[5][40][41] Following the release of the independent panel's review, the council decided to initiate a review of Fleischmann's and Pons's experiments, which would be overseen by council member and Utah State University professor Wilford Hansen.[40] According to The Salt Lake Tribune, an agreement had to be reached between university officials and the two researchers to have them turn over their research data to Hansen for review.[16]

In November, The Salt Lake Tribune reported that the institute had raised less than $100,000 ($246,000 in 2025) in private funding.[38] However, Will stated at the time that the institute was in discussions regarding private donations of over $4 million ($9.9 million in 2025).[42]
